On scaling matrices with partial row sums with prescribed bounds

$\mathbb{R}_+^{n\times n}$ denotes the set of $n\times n$ non-negative matrices. Let $ \mathbb{P}=\{P_1,\ldots,P_n\}$ be a set of n partitionings of $\{1,\ldots,n\}$. For $A\in\mathbb{R}_+^{n\times n}$ and each $i$, $P_i$ is a partitioning of the elements in the $i^{th}$ row of $A$ into partitions $\{p_{i,1}\ldots,p_{i,t_i}\}$ ( i.e. disjoint sets whose union is the set of elements in the $i^{th}$ row ) where $t_i$ is the number of partitions in the partitioning $P_i$. $\mathbb{K}$ denotes the set of constraints defined by specifying for the sum $S_{i,j}$ of the entries in each partition $p_{i,j}$ a lower bound $b^{(l)}_{i,j}$ and upper bound $ b^{(u)}_{i,j}$ so that $b^{(l)}_{i,j}\leq S_{i,j}\leq b^{(u)}_{i,j}$ for $1\leq i\leq n\ ,\ 1\leq j\leq t_i$.

Let $\Omega_{\mathbb{K}}=\{A\in\mathbb{R}_+^{n\times n}: A \text{ satisfies constraints } \mathbb{K}\}$. Techniques for calculating the interval $[\min_{A\in \Omega_{\mathbb{K}}} \rho(A), \max_{A\in \Omega_{\mathbb{K}}} \rho(A)]$ where $\rho$ denotes $\max_{x>0}\min_{i,x_i\neq 0}\frac{\sum_j a_{i,j}x_j}{x_i}$ are described.

Since $\{\rho(A):A\in\Omega_{\mathbb{K}}\}=[\min_{A\in \Omega_{\mathbb{K}}} \rho(A), \max_{A\in \Omega_{\mathbb{K}}} \rho(A)]$, the results have application for researchers seeking existence conditions for solutions to linear systems where some of the coefficients are unknown or vary with time.

Random reflections, foldings, and symmetrizations on the sphere

Two-point symmetrization is a simple rearrangement that has been used to give a short proof of the isoperimetric inequality on the sphere. For each reflection on the sphere, there is a two-point symmetrization that pushes mass across the great circle of symmetry towards the north pole. A key point is that the only sets invariant under all two-point symmetrizations are the polar caps. If a sequence of reflections is chosen uniformly at random, then the corresponding sequence of symmetrizations almost surely transforms every subset of the sphere to a polar cap.

If the reflections are chosen according to a distribution that is not i.i.d. uniform --- under what conditions is there still convergence? Are polar caps the only possible limits? What can be said about the rate of convergence?

Describing geometry and symmetry of cryo-EM datasets using algebra

Cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) is a microscopy technique used to discover the 3D structure of molecules from very noisy images. We discuss how algebra can describe two aspects of cryo-EM datasets.

First, we'll describe common lines datasets. Common lines are lines of intersection between cryo-EM images in 3D. They are a crucial ingredient in some 2D->3D reconstruction algorithms, and they can be characterized by polynomial equalities and inequalities. Second, we'll discuss how 3D symmetries of a molecule can be detected from only 2D cryo-EM images, without performing full 3D reconstruction.

This talk is a presentation to Eva Nogales' lab. The Nogales lab uses cryo-EM to investigate the structure of proteins. The math content of the talk is geared towards biophysicists, while mathematicians might enjoy learning a little bit about cryo-EM and some of the challenges it poses.
