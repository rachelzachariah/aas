Jan 31, 2014

Identifiability for Bilinear Inverse Problems with (Eventual) Applications to Blind Deconvolution, Matrix Factorization and Dictionary Finding

Motivated by channel estimation problems in underwater acoustic communications, sparse channel estimation is investigated. For a particular problem relevant to cooperative communications, we see that our estimation problem of interest can be mapped to a bilinear inverse problem. In fact, a number of important inverse problems in signal processing, such as blind deconvolution, matrix factorization, dictionary learning and blind source separation share the common characteristic of being bilinear inverse problems. In such problems, the observation model is a function of two variables and conditioned on one variable being known, the observation is a linear function of the other variable. A key question is that of identifiability: can one unambiguously recover the pair of inputs from the output? We shall consider both deterministic conditions for identifiability as well as probabilistic statements that result in new scaling laws under cone constraints. Our approach is based on a unifying and flexible approach to identifiability that exploits a connection to low-rank matrix recovery.

Bernie Lesieutre

University of Wisconsin - Madison

Apr 11, 2014

Calculating All the Solutions to the Power Flow Equations

In this seminar we review the problem of calculating all the (real-valued) solutions to the "power flow" equations. In the field of power engineering, the power flow equations relate network voltages to power generation and demand. These nonlinear equations are used in some form for almost every problem in power engineering. For most application only a single solution is of interest, however for some special problems it is desired to find all the solutions. We present one such problem involving the construction of bifurcation diagrams to study the propensity for cascading outages in distribution networks. We review the literature on the number and the nature of solutions to the power flow equations, discuss a long-accepted algorithm for calculation of all the solutions, and present a counter-example to this algorithm. We conclude with a discussion of our on-going research on this topic.

Dan Romik

UC Davis

Apr 11, 2014

The qKZ equation and some conjectural constant term identities, with applications to probability

In 1962, Freeman Dyson conjectured that the constant term in a certain multivariate Laurent polynomial arising in a problem in statistical physics is given by a multinomial coefficient. His conjecture was proved soon afterwards, but since then a large number of similar (but more complicated) "constant term identities" have been discovered. Some of them are notoriously difficult to prove and are related to famous problems in algebraic combinatorics like the enumeration of alternating sign matrices and various classes of plane partitions.

In this talk I will describe a new class of constant term identities, most of them conjectural, generalizing an identity proved in 2007 by Di Francesco, Zinn-Justin and Zeilberger. Through a complicated algebraic machinery known as the quantum Knizhnik-Zamolodchikov (qKZ) equation, I was recently able to show that these conjectures would imply a surprising result in probability, namely that the probability of so-called "connectivity events" in a certain percolation model are all rational numbers computable by an explicit algorithm. (The probabilistic aspect of the results will be described more in detail in my probability seminar talk the previous day.)

Nikki Meshkat

NC State

Apr 18, 2014

Identifiability of Linear Compartment Models

Identifiability concerns finding which unknown parameters of a model can be quantified from given input-output data. Many linear ODE models, used primarily in Systems Biology, are unidentifiable, which means that parameters can take on an infinite number of values and yet yield the same input-output data. We study a particular class of unidentifiable models and find conditions to obtain identifiable reparameterizations of these models. In particular, we use a graph-theoretic approach to analyze the models and show that graphs with certain properties allow a monomial scaling reparameterization over identifiable functions of the parameters. We also examine conditions to obtain local identifiability for this class of models, and in particular, show how identifiability can by determined by simply looking at the graphical structure of these linear compartment models.

Tyson Williams

University of Wisconsin - Madison

May 2, 2014

Siegel’s theorem, edge coloring, and a Holant dichotomy

What do Siegel’s theorem on finiteness of integer solutions have to do with complexity theory? In this talk, we discuss a new complexity dichotomy theorem for counting problems. Such a dichotomy is a classification of a class of problems into exactly two kinds: those that are computable in polynomial time, and those that are #P-hard, and thus intractable. An example problem in this dichotomy is the problem of counting the number of edge colorings of a graph. We will show that an effective version of Siegel’s theorem and some Galois theory are key ingredients in the proof of this dichotomy. Along the way we will also meet the Tutte polynomial, medial graphs, Eulerian partitions, Puiseux series, and a certain lattice condition on the (logarithms of) the roots of polynomials with integer coefficients. This is joint work with Jin-Yi Cai and Heng Guo.

Boaz Nadler

Weizmann Institute

May 9, 2014

Vectorial Phase Retrieval for 1-D signals and 2-D images

Phase retrieval - namely the recovery of a signal from its absolute Fourier transform is a problem of fundamental importance in many fields. In 1-D Phase retrieval is ill-posed, admitting multiple solutions. In 2-D it often admits a unique solution, but finding it is challenging. Current iterative algorithms are computationally intensive and most have no theoretical guarantees on the properties of their obtained solution.

In this talk I’ll present a novel framework, denoted vectorial phase retrieval. The key underlying idea is that in many physical scenarios one can measure not only one signal, but also another independent one, and their interference.

From a mathematical perspective, We show that this novel framework leads to uniqueness already in the 1-D case. We next derive computationally efficient and robust to noise algorithms for recovery of the phases under several 1-D and 2-D settings of practical physical interest.

We illustrate the reconstruction performance of our algorithms both with simulated signals, as well as on actual experimental 2-d data. We conclude with several yet unresolved challenges - mathematical, statistical and computational.
